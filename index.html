The bottleneck

Growth usually slows down where warehouse operations stay manual.

Growth hits the warehouse first

At first, a few deliveries, manual stock tracking, and self-managed packing look manageable. Then order volume, SKUs, returns, and marketplace rules expand faster than the process behind them.

The owner gets dragged into operations

Every day starts with the same questions: what arrived, what went missing, what shipped wrong, and whether today's dispatch will hold. The team puts out fires instead of building a repeatable system.

The root cause repeats

There is no single point of responsibility, no transparent stock control, no clear SLA for assembly and returns, and no reliable link between sales channels and real inventory movement.

Chaos is expensive

A labeling error, a mixed SKU, or one missed shipment can wipe out the margin of an entire batch. The warehouse stops supporting sales and starts taxing them.

Operating models

We adapt the warehouse model to the way you actually sell.

The flow can be built around marketplaces, direct orders, wholesale shipments, or a hybrid mix across them. The point is one controllable process instead of parallel manual work.

FBO

Fulfillment by Operator

You bring goods into the warehouse, and we take over prep, control, and channel-ready handoff so the stock enters an organized flow instead of a manual queue.

  • Receiving goods from the seller or supplier side
  • Checking appearance, completeness, labeling, and marketplace requirements
  • Preparing shipments for the required downstream channel

FBS

Fulfillment by Seller

Goods stay in the warehouse, we receive order data, assemble against SLA, label, pack, and dispatch on behalf of the seller without lag between order intake and fulfillment.

  • Storage and live stock control
  • Fast assembly and prep by order rules
  • Dispatch without handoff delays

DBS

Delivery by Seller

Goods can stay on our side or in a combined scheme. After the order is placed, we prepare and route delivery directly to the buyer without adding extra operational friction.

B2B

Business to Business

We handle wholesale and network shipments with pallet dispatch, documentation, schedules, labeling, and execution control across recurring corporate orders.

B2C

Business to Consumer

We support direct sales through online stores, social channels, and marketplaces: order intake, assembly, packing, delivery handoff, returns, and channel-level reporting.

FAQ

Questions teams ask before handing off warehouse operations.

At what volume does fulfillment start to make sense?

Not only at very high volume. If you already have regular inbound, several sales channels, and a team getting stuck in assembly and dispatch, an external warehouse layer is often cheaper than manual chaos.

How long does launch usually take?

It depends on SKU count, labeling rules, and process complexity. Simple projects move faster, while more complex ones need audit, test receiving, and operating setup before a realistic launch date is fixed.

Can we use only part of the service package?

Yes, but the best outcome usually comes from linking receiving, stock control, labeling, assembly, and dispatch. Warehouse problems rarely live in only one step, so we look at the economics as a full process.

How is pricing calculated?

Usually through several blocks: receiving, storage, product operations, assembly, packaging, dispatch, and returns. A transparent estimate before launch is more useful than a low first-line rate with hidden operating cost later.

Can you support special delivery routes or extra marketplace requirements?

That depends on product category, route, documents, and platform rules. Those cases are best calculated separately once the operational requirements are clear.
